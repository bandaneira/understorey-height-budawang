# understorey-height-budawang
Understorey height monitoring using a fusion of active and passive sensor in Budawang National Park.

The 2019–2020 Australian megafires caused extensive damage to forest ecosystems, highlighting the need for improved monitoring of understorey vegetation, which plays a critical role in fuel load dynamics and post-fire recovery. This study developed a methodology to estimate understorey height and its temporal changes in Budawang National Park, New South Wales, through the integration of spaceborne LiDAR, radar, and optical data. Global Ecosystem Dynamics Investigation (GEDI) data were used as reference for understorey height, while L-band ALOS PALSAR-2 and Sentinel-2 imagery served as predictor variables in a Random Forest regression model. The model achieved moderate accuracy (R² = 0.02 at point level, R²bins = 0.60, RMSE = 2.00 m) and identified optical sensor including red-edge, near-infrared, and short-wave infrared bands as the most influential predictors compared to radar sensor. Application of the model to 2020 (post-fire) and 2024 (rehabilitation) datasets revealed a clear recovery trend, with mean understorey height increasing from 3.2 ± 1.9 m to 4.9 ± 1.4 m. These findings demonstrate the potential of multi-sensor data fusion for characterizing vertical vegetation structure beneath the canopy and provide a scalable framework for monitoring fuel availability and ecological recovery across fire-affected landscapes.
